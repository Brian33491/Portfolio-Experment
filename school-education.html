Role UX Researcher & Designer
Timeline 3 Months

Project Overview

The original goal of the project was to improve the advising page for the SOE, aiming to increase student engagement and make sure students knew that advisors were always available to help. However, after conducting some research, we realized that the problem wasn't limited to just the advising page—it was the entire website. As a result, our project expanded from improving a single page to revamping the whole website.

Initial analysis of the School of Education website

Research & Discovery

We began by observing our target audience—students—and analyzing their behavior and device usage. I visited the library, the LA building, and the food court, where I found that the majority of students were using laptops or school PCs, with a smaller group using their phones and just one person on a tablet. Additionally, I observed that about 70% of students were female, while 30% were male.

Following the surveys, I interviewed Carlos, an SOE student aiming to graduate in Pre-Elementary Education. From our conversation, I learned that Carlos would visit the advising website if it contained useful information. However, he mentioned that he wouldn't visit the page for scheduling since he already used MyUVU for that purpose.

User Interviews & Eye Tracking

We conducted comprehensive user interviews combined with eye-tracking technology to understand how students interacted with the website. The eye-tracking data revealed that students' attention was scattered across the page without clear focal points, confirming our hypothesis about information overload.

The eye-tracking heatmaps showed that students spent most of their time trying to locate specific information rather than absorbing content, indicating poor information architecture.

Design Process

After presenting our findings to our clients, explaining how students struggled to find the advising page, we began the design phase. We conducted additional eye-tracking tests which showed that most students had difficulty navigating the website or felt overwhelmed by the information presented.

Our design approach focused on creating clear visual hierarchies and reducing cognitive load. We implemented card-based layouts for better information organization and added intuitive navigation elements.

Wireframes & Prototypes

After completing our research, I created wireframes with the goal of improving the Faculty And Staff, Livescan Fingerprinting, and Global Opportunities page. For the Faculty And Staff page, I added an additional navigation bar so students wouldn't have to scroll endlessly to find their advisors.

For Livescan, I organized the information into cards for better clarity, and I did the same for the Global Opportunities page. The card-based design allowed for better scanning of information and reduced visual clutter.

Usability Testing with Eye Tracking

For the final usability test, we used the same tasks from the initial eye-tracking test but with our redesigned interface. The results showed significant improvements in terms of students finding their way through the website and completing tasks 120% faster than with the original website.

The follow-up eye-tracking sessions demonstrated more focused attention patterns, with users spending less time searching for information and more time engaging with relevant content.

Final Presentation

We presented our work to the advisors and other SOE staff, who were impressed with our findings and the improvements we made. They appreciated how we addressed the issues with the website—such as students feeling overwhelmed by information—and the challenge of students giving up when trying to find their first-year advisors.

They particularly liked the updates, like having the first-year advisor listed on the SOE advising page, the addition of an FAQ section, and the creation of a new page called student resources.

Conclusion

This project taught me how much easier and faster a project can be when working with a team. I also learned just how much time is spent on research, which I found to be incredibly beneficial. What started as a small task grew into a much larger project, and I found that process really interesting.

The integration of eye-tracking technology provided invaluable insights that traditional research methods might have missed. It allowed us to see exactly how users interacted with the interface at a granular level.

Overall, this was one of my favorite projects to work on, and it was fun collaborating with others for the first time. The success of this project demonstrated the power of user-centered design combined with rigorous research methodologies.
